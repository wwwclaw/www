About

Here are some (mostly technical) details about me. Contact me if you want to know more.

Bio

I am a husband and father living in LA. I work as a software developer, and in my free time I like to research and write software in areas that interest me, like computer vision. I also like TV and movies, weightlifting, and going on hikes with my dog. I grew up on the east coast and went to Cornell where I studied Electrical and Computer Engineering (undergrad) and Systems Engineering (grad).

Motivation

I believe technology is advancing too quickly to be fully useful. Companies gather so much data, the valuable information is too hard to extract, and many consumer products are designed to keep a user's attention but not make their lives better.

I like to build things that make technology easier to use. For businesses I believe in making it easy to learn valuable lessons from data - which lines up with the vision of Conduce, where I work. For people, I believe in building tools that make it more natural to work with technology, or build tools that just do something useful without any setup or learning curve - technology that gives people time.

Technical Background

I started getting serious about technology in high school. I modified racing games by adding extra cars and race tracks. Then I started hacking them to make opponents faster or slower, and tuning the game to make it more fun. I was also obsessed with the Simpsons, and combined that with my love of computer. I had a collection of TV recordings that I transferred to my computer and started to get rid of antenna/VHS static by writing image processing code in C++. This mostly involved blurring the video over areas where the color was pretty constant - e.g. blurring the yellow regions, but not blurring with any black outline pixels.

In college I started out as a CS major. CS was taught using Java, which I didn't like compared to C/C++, so I switched majors. I found Electrical and Computer Engineering where I really liked signal processing, and I learned how to do things like build JPEGs, and send wireless signals. I never liked the pure digital problems, so I minored in Material Science, which is more abstract and "fuzzy".

When I graduated, I started working on radar systems at Lockheed Matin while pursuing my masters in Systems Engineering at Cornell. I learned how to work with complex systems and how to work with lots of data. A radar measures everything, and figuring out what is interesting versus what is background noise is challenging. I invented a few things there, including a algorithm for fusing image processing results, and a very elegant algorithm for tracking targets with only a couple data points in a noisy environment, which is used on the TPQ53. And that's about as much as I can say about that. Most of the work I did was in MATLAB where I mocked up algorithms, though I sometimes worked with the real code.

While I was at Lockheed I helped found a startup called GrafPad. This is where I learned to write real code instead of just mathematical models. I wrote a program that translated a set of touch coordinates into shapes for GrafPad - you can see it in action on the Projects page. Around this time, I also left Lockheed and joined a startup in Los Angeles/Pasadena called OpenX. At OpenX I was now programming full time, and I helped rewrite their monolithic API using a microservices architecture.

While I was at OpenX, a friend invited me to join a startup called Conduce, that worked on data visualizations. The mission at Conduce is to display a user's data in a way that lets data tell its story. The mission and values of Conduce lined up very nicely with my own, and the opportunity to work with such a great team was too good to pass up, and this is where I am now.

While at OpenX and Conduce, in my spare time I have been working on projects to build my Computer Vision knowledge. I have two tools, one to clean up a whiteboard image, and a second to vectorize a clean image. I'm not sure what I'll do with them yet other than continue sharpening my skills.
